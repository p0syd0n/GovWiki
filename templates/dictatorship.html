Dictatorships

 

 

A dictatorship is a system of government where one person selflessly volunteers to shoulder all the burdens of leadership indefinitely, for the good of the nation. In such systems, leaders are not bound by things like elections, since voting can be a real hassle, and it's much easier to maintain stability when the same person stays in charge for, say, a few decades or until they lose interest.

The leader is usually selected through a process known as "national destiny," where destiny happens to favor the same person over and over again. Once in power, they take on the role of protecting the country from foreign influence, particularly those sneaky journalists, environmental activists, or anyone else who might ask unnecessary questions. In fact, it’s often considered rude to question the leader, as their decisions are said to be so perfect that debating them could cause confusion.

In a dictatorship, the people are free to express their opinions, as long as those opinions happen to match the government’s official narrative, which is a relief because it saves everyone from the overwhelming task of thinking critically. State-run media helpfully filters all news to ensure it never causes panic by revealing things like economic issues, or, say, unpopular invasions. This way, the citizens can live comfortably, unaware of unimportant matters like dissent or protests, which tend to disappear quickly for everyone's safety.

Leadership in a dictatorship often extends beyond traditional government duties. For example, the leader might also oversee sports, cultural events, and even decide what qualifies as good modern art. Their wisdom is often commemorated with statues and murals, reminding everyone of the great things they’ve accomplished, like winning elections with a modest 98% of the vote—a statistic that impresses even the most skeptical mathematicians.

While democracy can be messy with its constant turnover, a dictatorship offers the convenience of predictable leadership—you know, the kind where a person’s term lasts until 2024, or 2036, or 2048, or whenever it’s deemed "in the national interest" for the leader to take a well-deserved break.
